The Global Digital Divide

Exploring how internet access relates to economic development, happiness, and global inequality around the world.

Internet Over Time Internet & GDP Internet & Happiness World Map

Project Overview

Technology access has become an important part of modern life, influencing communication, education, work, and access to information. However, internet access is not equally distributed across countries. This project explores how internet usage varies globally and how it relates to economic development and happiness.

I used data from the World Bank API and the World Happiness Report to create interactive visualizations. My goal is to better understand global inequalities in technology access and quality of life.

Internet Access Over Time

To better understand how technology access has changed over time, I created an interactive line plot showing average internet usage by region. Instead of only looking at one global average, this visualization makes it easier to compare how different parts of the world have changed over time.

The graph shows that internet access has increased dramatically across all regions, especially after the early 2000s. However, the pace of growth is not equal. Regions such as North America and Europe reached high levels of internet access earlier, while regions such as Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia still lag behind. This suggests that the digital divide has narrowed over time, but important inequalities remain.

Internet Access and GDP per Capita

After looking at global trends over time, I examined whether internet access is related to economic development. This interactive scatterplot compares GDP per capita and internet access in 2024. Each point represents a country, and the colors show World Bank income-level groups.

The scatterplot shows a clear positive relationship between GDP per capita and internet access. In general, countries with higher GDP per capita tend to have a larger percentage of internet users. High-income countries cluster near very high internet access rates, while low-income countries tend to have lower access overall. However, the pattern is not perfect, suggesting that other factors such as infrastructure, policy, and education may also influence access.

Internet Access and Happiness

Economic development does not fully explain quality of life, so I also explored the relationship between internet access and happiness scores. This interactive scatterplot compares national happiness scores with internet access levels in 2024.

The visualization suggests a general positive relationship: countries with higher internet access often report higher happiness scores. High-income countries tend to appear near the upper-right area of the graph, while lower-income countries are more likely to appear in the lower-left. However, the relationship is not perfectly consistent. This shows that internet access may be associated with happiness, but it does not fully explain it. Other factors such as income, health, social support, and political stability also likely play important roles.

Switchable World Map

To bring the datasets together, I created an interactive world map that allows users to switch between internet access, GDP per capita, and happiness scores for 2024. This makes it easier to compare geographic patterns across different indicators.

The map shows that regions with high internet access often overlap with higher GDP and higher happiness levels. At the same time, the map also reveals important exceptions, showing that global inequality is complex. Technology access, economic development, and quality of life are connected, but no single variable can explain everything.

Conclusion

Overall, these visualizations suggest that global internet access has improved significantly over time, but strong inequalities remain between countries and regions. Internet access is closely related to economic development and appears to be associated with happiness, but it is only one part of a much larger global inequality story.
