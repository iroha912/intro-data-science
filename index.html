The Internet Is Growing Everywhere — But Not Equally

Internet access has become part of everyday life, but the ability to connect is still deeply unequal. This project explores where the digital divide remains, how it connects to wealth, and whether internet access also relates to national well-being.

World Map Change Over Time Wealth Happiness Takeaways

Why Internet Access Matters

Nowadays, the internet is more than entertainment or social media. It affects how people study, work, communicate, access information, and participate in society. Because of this, unequal internet access can reflect larger global inequalities.

Using data from the World Bank API and the World Happiness Report, this project explores how internet access relates to economic development and happiness across countries. Rather than simply presenting statistics, the goal is to help readers explore global patterns and inequalities through data.

The World Has Become More Connected

Internet access has increased dramatically across the world. However, different regions did not reach high levels of access at the same time.

Try this: look for which regions reached high internet usage earliest and which regions are still catching up.

Main insight

Internet access increased in every region, but growth has not been equal. Regions such as North America and Europe reached high internet access much earlier, while Sub-Saharan Africa still remains far below the global average. The digital divide has narrowed, but it has not disappeared.

Wealth Explains Part of the Divide

One possible explanation for unequal internet access is economic development. Countries with more resources may be better able to build infrastructure, expand networks, and make technology more affordable.

Try this: hover over countries that do not follow the main trend. What might explain these exceptions?

Main insight

Countries with higher GDP per capita usually have higher internet access. However, the relationship is not perfectly linear. Among high-income countries, internet access begins to level off because many countries have already reached near-universal internet usage.

Does Internet Access Mean a Better Life?

Economic development is important, but it does not fully describe people’s quality of life. To look beyond money, I compared internet access with national happiness scores.

Try this: look for countries with similar internet access but different happiness scores.

Main insight

Countries with high internet access often also report higher happiness scores. However, middle-income countries are much more widely scattered, suggesting that internet access alone cannot fully explain happiness. Other factors such as healthcare, social support, political stability, and inequality likely also play important roles.

How I Built This Project

I collected country-level data from the World Bank API and combined it with happiness data from the World Happiness Report . After cleaning country names, removing missing values, and matching countries across datasets, I created interactive visualizations to compare global patterns.

The main challenge was making different datasets work together. Country names, missing values, and different formats made the merging process difficult, but cleaning the data carefully was necessary before making meaningful comparisons.

What This Reveals

Overall, the data shows a positive relationship between internet access, GDP per capita, and happiness scores. Countries with higher internet access often also have stronger economies and higher reported well-being, although the pattern is not perfectly consistent.

These patterns suggest that internet access is closely connected to broader social and economic development. Access to technology may influence education, communication, and economic opportunity, while also reflecting existing global inequalities. Although the digital divide has narrowed over time, many regions are still left behind, showing that unequal access to technology remains an important global issue.
